Push S to zero: the color reaches white — but L must rise to log₂3. Push S to max: maximum saturation — but L drops to its minimum. You cannot change one without the other. This is the conservation law S + L = log₂3.
Theory
S + L = log₂3 — exact algebraic identity (GFT, D02). Saturation S = D_KL(π‖u) is the Kullback-Leibler divergence from white. Luminance L = H(π) is the Shannon entropy of the chromatic distribution. Their sum is constant for any color on the simplex. Increasing one must decrease the other. Analogy: free energy + heat = total energy (1st law of thermodynamics). CIE has no equivalent conservation law.

The Koide Point

Which saturation level is the most pleasing? A real perceptual test: choose among N patches across multiple random hues, then compare your average to the Koide prediction.

In 3- and 5-patch modes, no patch is pinned to 70.7%. Only 7-patch mode contains an exact Koide swatch.

Theory
The Koide identity (lepton masses, Q = 2/3) implies an optimal saturation at S_Koide = S_max / √2 ≈ 70.7% of maximum (D17b). This is the geometric mean between white (S=0) and a spectral primary (S=S_max). Independent perceptual studies converge to this range: Ou et al. (2004): 68–74%, Schloss & Palmer (2010): 65–75%, Munsell atlas: 70±5%. The CIE provides no explanation for this clustering; SCS derives it from the sieve structure.

Skin Tone Protection

Boosting saturation is common in photo editing. HSL treats all pixels the same — skin tones drift to orange. SCS boosts along the simplex geodesic: colors get more vivid while preserving the hue direction.

+100%

HSL (uniform boost)

Uniform S multiplication: skin tones shift to orange/red as saturation increases. The hue is not preserved.
Original

SCS (geodesic boost)

Boost along the simplex ray: the hue angle θ is preserved, luminance Y is constant. Skin tones stay natural.

Photo: Anna Shvets / Pexels (free license)

Theory
HSL boosts saturation by multiplying S uniformly: S' = min(1, S·(1+b)). This changes the hue because HSL saturation mixes the color toward gray along a linear path in RGB — not a perceptually uniform path. The SCS boost moves each pixel along its simplex ray (from center W toward the boundary), scaling the deviation π − u by factor (1+b). This preserves the hue angle θ = arctan(√3(π₅−π₇)/(2π₃−π₅−π₇)) exactly, and the luminance Y is held constant. The Fisher metric weights γ_p create a natural damping near the simplex edges — high-saturation pixels get less additional boost, protecting already-vivid skin tones. Section 11.4 of the paper.

Observe two things: (1) The yellow–blue median (midpoint of RG → B) follows the line π₃=π₅ (equal L and M cone weights) — the simplex naturally recovers the yellow–blue opponent axis of color vision, without imposing it. (2) The gradient contracts toward the vertices: colors change rapidly near the center W and slowly near the edges. This is the Fisher metric at work — chromatic discrimination is maximal at the white point and collapses at spectral primaries, exactly as MacAdam ellipses show experimentally.
Theory
The simplex Δ² is the probability 2-simplex: any color is a distribution (π₃, π₅, π₇) over the 3 active channels. The Fisher metric ds² = Σ γ_p · dπ_p²/π_p is the unique invariant metric (Čencov, 1982). The γ_p are not fitted: γ₃=0.808, γ₅=0.696, γ₇=0.596, derived from μ*=15. The hierarchy γ₃ > γ₅ > γ₇ reproduces the cone response ordering L > M > S. The Koide circle marks S = 70.7% of maximum.

Theory
CIELAB measures difference after an empirical cube-root remapping. SCS splits the comparison into two derived pieces: a luminance geodesic on Bernoulli space and a chromatic geodesic on the simplex. Theory
The practical form of the space is SCS(ℓ ; π₃, π₅, π₇), where ℓ is luminance relative to white and (π₃, π₅, π₇) is a probability distribution on the three active channels. The same color can also be written as SCS(ℓ ; S, θ), with saturation S = D_KL(π‖u) and hue angle θ. Important: ℓ is the physical luminance term, while L = H(π) is a chromatic entropy on the simplex. On a neutral axis (black, gray, white), the simplex stays at π = u, so L stays maximal even though ℓ changes.

Theory
The logic is explicit. First, the two raw captures stay visible to anchor the inter-sensor baseline. Then the source raw is graded with a real HSL module: master hue, saturation and lightness, plus six hue-band corrections. The resulting per-pixel delta from source raw to source grade is then transported onto the target raw in two coordinate systems: directly in HSL, or through the SCS simplex and luminance coordinates. The comparison asks which transport preserves the graded intent more coherently across sensors.
